ABOUT ME

Hi, my name is Hanh! Welcome to my page where I will attemt to bore you with my long life-story and some of my personal art. Ready?

Twenty years ago, if I told an adult I wanted to be an artist because I was passionate about art, they would have laughed at me. They would ask what I knew about passion at such young age, and I would be unable to answer. Perhaps I found my passion for art by gazing at my father’s brush strokes for hours before my mother told me to go to sleep. Or perhaps it started with the pure feeling of happiness looking at my doodles being displayed on the fridge. Growing up in Vietnam in a tiny one bedroom house where I shared a room with my father, mother and brother, I witnessed magic happed every day when my parents turned white paper into masterpieces, captured real life objects with only a canvas, brushes and a few bottles of paint. I cannot remember a day of my childhood without a crayon, a piece of paper, and an effort to create my own magic.

But life is not magic. “Life is a rocky road with many crossroads. Passion is your map, follow it, determination is your ride, it will get you there,” my mother would often tell me. I did not fully understand her words until my rocky road found me two days after my 15th birthday, when my biological parents showed up at the door in Vietnam, and I was convinced that it was my opportunity to pursue a better future in a developed country. Four months later, I arrived in Los Angeles, California, and within a month, I was a new student at La Puente High School. I was unprepared and had to face the biggest challenge - For the first time in my life, I experienced racism. At that time, I could only speak Vietnamese in an entirely English-speaking school. Due to the language barrier, I was cut off from the rest of society. I soon became the target of daily bullying by classmates and sometimes, even teachers. The difficulties of transitioning to the United States seemed insurmountable. The culture shock and cruelty at school was a heavy burden for not only me, but also my parents, whose work caused them to be absent often. Eventually, I could take no more and returned to Vietnam to stay with my grandmother. Despise the pain and embarrassment, the experience gave me a renewed ambition to learn English; so I could pursue my artistic passion in the United States.

With a drive to succeed, I returned to the States a year later only to be struck with yet another barrier. According to California law, I could not go back to high school, because I had already turned 18 years old. Thus, I moved to Seattle, Washington where I lived with my aunt who was teaching Vietnamese courses at the University of Washington. I attended Chief Sealth High School, and what I found there was simply amazing. Not only was the school diverse, but there was also a Vietnamese-Chinese club, which I soon joined and volunteered in the community, enabling me to gain experiences and broaden my cultural spectrum. Finally, my hard work to learn English had been rewarded.

My journey from the doodles on the fridge in Vietnam to the art classrooms at University of Washington began with my enrollment to the Associate of Arts Transfer Degree at South Seattle Community College. During my time in college, I worked part-time as a publication commissioner at the Women’s Center through my work-study. My view of academia and the United States expanded as I met many new people from a variety of cultures, people like me who were having difficulty building a life in a country where their mother languages were not understood. Not only was I fortunate enough to be exposed to a variety of human characters, enabling me to understand different patterns of thought, which set individuals apart, I also learned about myself through a different perspective, taking my cultural pride to a more knowledgeable level. Finally, I stopped feeling embarrassed about my own accent and found a home in the state of Washington.
